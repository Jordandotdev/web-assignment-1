Turtle Categories

The coastal areas of Sri Lanka are home to a diverse range of turtle species.These turtles, each with their own distinct qualities, contribute to the rich biodiversity and natural attraction of Sri Lanka's marine environments.These turtles are exceptional swimmers, managing the ocean currents with their streamlined bodies and powerful flippers. Furthermore, these turtles exhibit exceptional nesting patterns, returning to the same beaches year after year to lay their eggs. Sri Lankan turtles symbolize perseverance and flexibility, flourishing in coastal areas while playing an important part in the island's delicate marine ecosystems.

Green Turtle

The Sri Lankan green turtle (Chelonia mydas) is an attractive and iconic animal that captivates both nature enthusiasts and conservationists. The green turtle, known for its gorgeous emerald-colored carapace, is one of the world's largest marine turtle species, reaching astounding sizes. These beautiful creatures play an important role in the marine ecosystem, grazing on seagrasses and algae to help keep coastal habitats healthy.

Carnivores predominate among young green turtles. Adults, however, are herbivores that use their sharply serrated jaws to only consume marine flora. They can weigh up to 250 kg and reach a maximum length of 1m. A mature female will typically lay 120–140 eggs at a cycle. Green turtles depend on the coastal regions of Sri Lanka for their nesting grounds, and there are numerous communities with dense populations. The southernmost town on the island, Rekawa, is well known for having a sizable number of green turtle nests. A similar hotspot for green turtle nesting activity is Kosgoda, which is situated along the southwest coast.

These locations generate interest from academics, environmentalists, and tourists alike since they provide chances to see the nesting procedure and support efforts to maintain and conserve the green turtle species. Despite challenges like habitat loss and illicit poaching, concerted conservation initiatives are under work in these places and others to safeguard the green turtle species and guarantee their survival for future generations. A truly amazing experience that highlights the beauty and value of these wonderful creatures in Sri Lanka's marine ecology is seeing the grandeur of the green turtle in its native home in these coastal locations.

Hawksbill turtle

Even rare than the Green Turtle is the severely endangered Hawksbill Turtle. Additionally, it is significantly smaller, with a maximum length of 90 cm and a weight range of 50–70 kg. The hawksbill's tiny head and bird-like beak, which are used to hunt creatures hidden in small cracks, are what gave them their English name. It frequently travels to tropical and subtropical waters, including Sri Lanka. Despite being one of the lesser sea turtle species, this one is recognized for having a stunning shell that is composed of 13 symmetrical sections and is incredibly colorful.

Sri Lanka is lucky to have a number of areas with dense populations of hawksbill turtles. The southernmost part of the island's Rekawa beach is one such location. The importance of this shoreline as a hawksbill turtle nesting area has been recognized. Protecting the nesting areas and spreading awareness of the need of maintaining these ecosystems are the main o bjectives of conservation activities in Rekawa. To leave a lasting memory of the wonders of nature, visitors can see the amazing spectacle of hawksbill turtles emerging from the water to lay their eggs. In addition to Rekawa, Pigeon Island, located off the eastern coast of Sri Lanka, is another known site where hawksbill turtles thrive.

Hawksbill Turtle

Hawksbill turtles' existence in Sri Lanka's coastal waters is evidence of the nation's dedication to wildlife conservation as well as the importance of these efforts on a worldwide scale. Sri Lanka demonstrates its commitment to the preservation of biodiversity and the sustainable management of its marine resources by protecting the nesting sites, spreading awareness about the significance of hawksbill turtles, and offering opportunities for visitors to interact with these amazing creatures. Sri Lanka hopes to safeguard the long-term survival of hawksbill turtles through these communal efforts and to foster a profound respect for their outstanding beauty and ecological importance.

Leather-back Turtle

The largest of the five kinds of turtle, which is critically endangered and rare find in Sri Lanka. It is still in danger of going extinct. Long front flippers and a distinctive shell with black and white stripes make it easy to recognize; the carapace, which gives the creature a leathery look, is actually a layer of thick, rubbery skin dotted with hundreds of bone plates. In actuality, it is the only sea turtle that does not have a hard shell. Additionally, despite being a cold-blooded reptile, they have a special blood circulation system that enables them to use the metabolic heat generated by their muscle activity to keep their blood warm even in chilly waters.
